From the Director's Desk

The memory supercycle is here. Is your organization ready for it?

HBM has rewritten the economics of the fab. 3D DRAM is rewriting the physics. CXL is rewriting the server. What all three have in common is that they are, first and foremost, rewriting what we hire, train, and promote for.

By the Technical Director, Memory Technologies ◆ 8 min read ◆ Published April 23, 2026

Fig. 01 — HBM4 reference architecture. 16 stacked DRAM dies, TSMC-fabricated logic base, 2048-bit interface. Every chip here represents roughly 3× the wafer area of a conventional DDR5 module.

For thirty years, memory was the commodity corner of semiconductors — the part of the industry that lived and died by boom-and-bust cycles, where price curves slid reliably down the learning curve and the only strategic question most boards asked was how many bits per wafer we could squeeze out next quarter. That world is gone. In its place we have something the industry has never quite experienced before: a structural reset in which memory has become the single most contested resource in computing.

The numbers are now familiar to anyone who has read an earnings call this year. Micron's HBM capacity is sold out through all of calendar 2026. Server DRAM contract prices rose 55–60% quarter-over-quarter in Q1. NAND is up 33–38% in the same window. Memory makers are posting gross margins above 50%, the kind of number we used to associate with logic, not DRAM. And underneath all of it sits a physical reality that we, as technologists, must make visible to everyone in our organizations: producing one HBM chip consumes roughly three times the wafer area of a conventional DDR5 module. Every HBM ramp is a DDR5 shortage. Every AI server we ship is a laptop that doesn't.

This editorial is not another market outlook. There are enough of those. This one is about what I believe is the harder, more consequential question for anyone running a memory or memory-adjacent organization today: we can see the technology frontier clearly — HBM4, 3D DRAM, CXL, near-memory computing — but do the people in our organizations, and the goals we are pointing them at, actually match the frontier we say we want to build?

01 / The Technology Frontier

Three simultaneous inflections, and why they matter together

I want to sketch the frontier in a single paragraph, because too often we talk about these shifts as isolated product roadmaps when in reality they are one interlocking story. HBM4 moved into high-volume production in Q1 this year; 16-Hi 48 GB stacks are scheduled for late 2026. 3D DRAM — vertical-gate, stacked-cell architectures using Si/SiGe materials — is shifting the core DRAM bit cell off the planar shrink treadmill for the first time in a generation. CXL 3.0 has quietly graduated from experiment to essential plumbing; when you can't buy enough HBM, the only other answer is to pool DDR5 across a rack and let the accelerators reach across the fabric. And sitting next to all of this, NAND has cleared 300 layers with hybrid bonding, and QLC enterprise SSDs have overtaken client SSDs as the largest NAND segment for the first time in the market's history.

Key Insight

The transition from 12-Hi to 16-Hi HBM is technically harder than the jump from 8 to 12.

Wafer thickness has to drop from roughly 50 µm to 30 µm while preserving structural integrity and thermal performance. This is the kind of problem that isn't solved by a single heroic team — it requires packaging, process, materials, and thermal engineering moving in lockstep. If your org chart still treats those as separate kingdoms, the roadmap will slip.

None of these transitions is complete on its own. HBM4 without a base-die logic co-design story is just a faster version of HBM3E. 3D DRAM without EUV patterning maturity is a demo. CXL without a memory-tiering software stack is a spec sheet. The organizations that are going to lead the next decade are the ones that grasp, at every level, that memory is no longer a component — it is a system. And systems require a different kind of team than components do.

02 / The Organizational Imperative

Goals must shift from volume to value, and from silos to systems

Here is the uncomfortable truth I have been repeating in our quarterly reviews: most memory organizations — ours included, until recently — were optimized for a world that no longer exists. The KPIs we inherited measured bits shipped, yield percentage on the mature node, and cost-per-gigabit. Those metrics are not wrong, but they are no longer sufficient. They were designed for a commodity. We are no longer shipping a commodity.

When Micron's CEO says HBM is "more than sold out," the strategic question for leadership is not how to extract a few more percentage points of yield out of a legacy line. It is: are we allocating our scarcest resource — engineering attention — to the products that define the next five years, or to the products that defined the last five? Every head of memory that I speak with is wrestling with some version of this reallocation problem. Most are underestimating how hard it is.

If your roadmap is HBM4 and CXL, but your compensation plan still pays out on DDR4 yield, the roadmap is going to lose. — A principle I've watched play out three times in my career

Alignment is not a slogan. It is a set of concrete, mundane design choices about how an organization runs. In the memory business right now, I see four specific areas where misalignment is silently costing companies the future:

1. The incentive-to-roadmap gap

Ask your managers what percentage of their bonus is tied to HBM/advanced packaging outcomes versus legacy DRAM. If the ratio doesn't match your stated capital allocation, you have a goal-alignment problem regardless of how many HBM slides are in the strategy deck.

2. The hiring-to-technology gap

The skills that built planar DRAM — shrink discipline, cell-level optimization, photolithography mastery — are necessary but no longer sufficient. HBM and 3D DRAM demand packaging engineers, thermal co-design specialists, TSV integration experts, base-die logic co-designers, and interposer/substrate process owners. In most memory companies, these roles are still treated as adjacencies rather than the core of the business they now are.

3. The org-chart-to-customer gap

HBM is now a custom product. NVIDIA, Google TPU, AWS Trainium and Meta's MTIA all want different base-die logic, different thermal envelopes, different signaling. Organizations structured around standard-product lines cannot serve this customer-specific future without a fundamental redesign of how customer engineering and product engineering interact.

4. The training-to-frontier gap

Your mid-career engineers — the ones hired in the 2015-2020 window — were trained on a memory industry that no longer exists. They are not obsolete; they are under-invested-in. Every quarter a company does not spend real money on re-skilling this cohort is a quarter the frontier moves further away from them.

03 / What We Are Hiring and Training For

The skills matrix for the memory organization of 2026

When I am asked by early-career engineers, university programs, or internal mobility candidates what skills will matter most in memory over the next five years, this is the honest answer I give. It is not exhaustive, but it is the lens I apply to almost every hiring decision and every training budget I sign off on.

Pillar 01

Advanced Packaging & Heterogeneous Integration

TSV, hybrid bonding, 2.5D/3D interposer design, chiplet integration, CoWoS-class packaging. The single highest-leverage skill area in memory today.

Pillar 02

Thermal & Signal Co-Design

At 16 layers and 2.8 TB/s, thermal and SI/PI are no longer afterthoughts. Engineers who can work across the mechanical–electrical boundary are disproportionately valuable.

Pillar 03

Systems & Architecture Literacy

Understanding the workload. Memory engineers who can read an LLM inference trace or a CXL fabric topology will design better products than ones who cannot.

Pillar 04

AI & ML for Fab Operations

Yield modelling, defect classification, process-parameter optimization. Every memory fab is becoming an AI-instrumented fab. Fluency with the models themselves is now a line skill, not a specialist one.

Pillar 05

Software-Defined Memory & CXL

Memory tiering, pooling, coherency, the software contract between accelerator and memory fabric. Very few classical memory engineers are comfortable here yet. That is the opportunity.

Pillar 06

Customer-Specific Product Engineering

HBM4 is a custom product. Engineers who can partner with a hyperscaler on base-die customization — and navigate IP, confidentiality, and co-development in parallel — are now indispensable.

04 / A Leadership Charge

What I am asking of every leader on my team this quarter

I will close with the specific ask I have been making of my own leadership team, because I think it generalizes. If you are a director, VP, or team lead in a memory or memory-adjacent organization, I would urge you to run the following four-step audit before the end of this quarter. Not as a strategy exercise — as an operational one.

First, map your current compensation and promotion incentives against your five-year technology roadmap. Where the two disagree, the incentives will win. Fix the incentives, not the roadmap.

Second, take the six skill pillars above, or your own version of them, and run a real headcount analysis. Not "do we have packaging engineers," but "do we have enough packaging engineers at the seniority required, in the locations required, with the customer exposure required?" The honest answer, in almost every organization I benchmark against, is no.

Third, invest — genuinely, with real budget and real time — in re-skilling your existing mid-career bench. They are the ones who know your fabs, your customers, your institutional knowledge. They are not the problem. Not re-investing in them is the problem.

Fourth, and most importantly: make the strategic story legible. Every technician on the shop floor, every process engineer on the 1c node, every reliability engineer on an automotive qualification flow should be able to tell you, in one sentence, how their work connects to the company's position in the AI memory supercycle. If they can't, that is a leadership failure, not an employee one.

The Core Argument

We are at the beginning of a decade-long memory transition, not at its end.

HBM3E gives way to HBM4, then HBM4E, then HBM5 by 2029 and — if the JEDEC roadmap holds — HBM8 by 2038. The companies that will lead that decade are the ones that align their organizational goals, their compensation, their hiring, and their training with where the technology is going, not where it has been.

The memory business spent thirty years being a commodity. It will spend the next decade being the most strategically important component in computing. That is extraordinary news for our industry. Whether it is extraordinary news for your specific organization depends, almost entirely, on choices you are making right now about people. The bits will follow.
